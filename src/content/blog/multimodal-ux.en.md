People usually talk about multimodal models as a checklist: "does images, does audio, does video." For a product, something else matters more — they remove the most expensive element of an interface: manual input.

## A form is a tax on the user

Every form asks a human to translate reality into fields. Photograph a receipt and type in the amount, date, category by hand. Look at a plate and find each item in a database. That translation is work, and the user pays for it with time and patience.

A multimodal model takes the translation on itself. The user supplies raw material — a photo, voice, a screenshot — and the model extracts the structure.

## How it looks in NURA

The old food-tracking flow: open the app, search "oatmeal," pick from ten options, enter grams, repeat for the banana, repeat for the almonds. Five steps, a minute, annoyance.

The new flow: photograph the plate. That's it.

I didn't add a feature to the form — I **deleted the form**. Those are different things. When input costs one action instead of twenty, retention doesn't move by percentages, it moves by multiples.

## Where the catch is

- The model gets things wrong, and the user needs an easy way to correct the estimate — without that, trust evaporates.
- The "magic" only works if it's fast: a five-second delay kills the sense of instant.
- Raw input has to be validated — a photo of a dish might turn out to be a photo of a cat.

The takeaway for builders: look at your forms as a list of jobs a multimodal model can take over. The strongest feature of 2026 is often not a new button, but a form that disappeared.
